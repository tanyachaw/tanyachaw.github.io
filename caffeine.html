Caffeine

8/13/24

There are days where you tell yourself you will quit caffeine and for the first time in your life, you get a decaf americano and call it growth.

Then one day, you surrender.

You walk to the Old Brooklyn Bagel Shoppe and wonder why it’s spelled “shoppe.” You get the same bagel your mom called her best meal in New York despite you both going to Junoon the previous day. You try eating it in the sun until two bees assault your bagel and you run away.

You get an americano from a bakery across the street. You sit outside with the coffee and try eating the bagel again but a bigger bee arrives so you run again.

Lulu Miller, your boss and a gem of a human being, wrote a book called ‘Why Fish Don’t Exist’ about David Starr Jordan. Jordan, she wrote, “eschew[ed] not just booze and tobacco but even caffeine, for its dangerous ability to alter perception.”

You walk to Prospect park. You’re in your head and suddenly you see someone you know. You squint to make sure it’s them, say hello. If you didn’t know them personally, you’d think they were put together.

You sit facing the Grand Army Plaza arch and think of how much you miss Irani cafes. How much you would give up to go to an Irani Cafe and lose all willpower to a bun maska and chai despite your lactose intolerance which is actually not an intolerance but a sensitivity that you named an intolerance. You also know that after you visit an Irani cafe, the bun won't be as good as you remembered. The bun hasn't changed though, you have. You don't miss the bun, you miss the version of yourself who enjoyed it.

Caffeine is a plant alkaloid, which we should define.

We know what plants are (or do we? Not really, but for the sake of sanity, picture a green thing). An alkaloid is a compound that contains at least one nitrogen atom. A compound is a substance made of two or more elements. An element is made up of several atoms of the same type. An atom is “the basic building block of matter,” but a more rigorous definition is that it’s the smallest part of a substance that can’t be broken down chemically. Matter is anything that takes up space and can be weighted, i.e. has volume and mass.

There’s a class of kids running around the park. You look at them and understand that they are much smarter than you’ll ever be. That you lost so much charm and wisdom growing up. That they know much more about life, cooperation, and humility than you probably ever will now.

Coffee blocks adenosine receptors. Let’s just say it’s a molecule and neurotransmitter that regulates sleep and wakefulness. In the heart, caffeine increases coronary blood flow. In the lungs, it can cause muscle relaxation and bronchial dilatation, which is the widening of airways in the lungs. It’s antiasthmatic, which means it can be used to help treat asthma. Obviously it isn’t a replacement for asthma medication but the increased airflow helps with shortness of breath. It also stimulates the gastrocolic reflex which promotes bowel movements, but we knew that already.

You remember a child in Berkeley. She boarded your bus like a beam of light and told the conductor to not let her mom in. “Close the door on her!” Then she came up to you and gave you a flower. Or the carcass of a flower. You held on to it smiling for the rest of the way to campus. You would not like to be a kid again though.

You finish your bagel and get up to leave fully knowing your right leg is asleep. You walk ten steps and your leg stops working. You hold on to a fence for a couple minutes or was it ten seconds?

Stimulatory action of caffeine includes releasing dopamine (reward and pleasure), norepinephrine (alertness), serotonin (mood regulation), acetylcholine (memory and learning), glutamate (excitement), and GABA (helps calm neural activity and prevent excessive excitement). OK! What about the jitters? It’s the blocking of adenosine that causes the excessive, too excessive, alertness.

Instead of going home, you walk to Unnameable Books and impulsively buy an instructional book. Then a beautiful cookbook called Fresh India. 15 dollars!

Withdrawal symptoms include headaches, irritability, nervousness, and a reduction in energy.

Later in the day you find out that your taxi driver, Ramon, is from the Dominican Republic and moved to New York in 1999. He used to drive for Uber Eats during Covid, during which he thought New York looked like the Walking Dead. You’re jittery from the withdrawal and trying to focus on the conversation. The two of you discuss how maybe some bats in China shut down the entire world in 2020. “It was worse than World War II,” he says.

He tells you how the IRS is actually the most powerful entity in the United States. How they took away his cousin’s passport after he didn’t pay his taxes. How the government knows us better than we do.

This book chapter ends with this sentence: “caffeine does not have a convincing profile as an addictive drug.”

He thinks Trump will win because America isn’t really for a female president yet. America wasn’t ready for Hillary, he argues, it’s definitely not ready for Kamala. He concludes that people like Trump so much because of his lack of fear.

Your jitters are gone. Now you’re asking him if the mayor of New York is Rudy Giuliani and he’s laughing at you.

Ramon does not like the current mayor of New York City. He thinks he’s “soft” and doesn’t deliver on his promises.

Because of neuroplasticity, the neural pathways of a brain can change. You can’t undo them, but you can override them. You tell yourself you don’t like coffee enough times, and you’ll start acting that way. Buying that americano was hard. Decaf is fine, dandelion root tea is fine.
