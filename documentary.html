I've been speaking to documentary filmmakers on their process. Here are notes.

Matthew Busch

Matthew's a photographer, cinematographer, filmmaker, and journalist - the whole gamut. He's getting his masters from the Berkeley J-school in documentary film. I saw Matthew's work at an India Reporting event during my last days at Cal. His documentary was about a man in Pushkar, Rajasthan who called himself the "Indian Deepfaker" because of his job creating AI content on behalf of politicians for the upcoming Indian election cycle.

The documentary was ridiculously good. Then I saw his work online which was also ridiculously good. His photos are raw, intimate, ethereal, almost unreal. His portraits of a Mandaean community in Texas are like paintings. There's one picture he took in Poland of two couples kissing. You can feel the intimacy of the photo. And I was so baffled at how he managed to pull off being a fly on a wall during something like that.

I spoke with him a few weeks after. Here are a couple things we talked about.

Build relationships with the people you're shooting.

This doesn't apply every time, but Matthew worked on the Mandaean project for over 5 years. He was curious about it and showed up one day to ask around. This community has been ostracized in the past, so approaching them saying "hi I wanna take pictures of you for an amount of time" wasn't going to be effective. Then he began very human work - cultivating trust and relationships. A lot of it is feeling it out, and eventually asking them if you can take a photo of them.

People will say no, and that's fine.
